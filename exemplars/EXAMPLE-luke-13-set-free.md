## 01 — Brief

- **Date / occasion:** an ordinary Sunday, all-age service, no special season.
- **Text:** Luke 13:10–17 — Jesus heals a woman bent over for eighteen years, on the Sabbath, and the synagogue leader objects.
- **Length:** ~15 minutes.
- **Audience:** small congregation, mix of long-timers and a few newer faces; a couple of people carrying long-term illness.
- **Series:** none.
- **Notes:** there's an allotment-harvest theme to the morning's hymns; fine to lean into ordinary, earthy imagery.

---

## 02 — Devotional (honest notes, no commentaries yet)

Read it out loud three mornings running. What keeps landing:

- **Eighteen years.** Not a sudden thing. A long, ground-down, learned-to-live-with-it thing. She's not even asking. She's just *there*, on a Sabbath, like always.
- **"Bent over, and could not straighten up at all."** I kept feeling that in my own back. The way worry folds you. I've had stretches — the first winter here, knowing nobody — where I was bent over like that on the inside. Doing sums at 3am. Couldn't have told you the last time I looked up.
- **He sees her first.** She doesn't push through the crowd. "When Jesus saw her, he called her over." That undid me a bit. Being *seen* before you've performed your need.
- **The leader is cross about the timing.** And Jesus is having none of it — "ought she not be set free… on the sabbath day?"

Four works, what I genuinely hear:
- **Encounter:** strong. She meets God-in-Christ as the one who notices the overlooked.
- **Good news:** "set free" — release, not a tidy-up.
- **Worldview:** the Sabbath is *for* this. Rules that exist to protect rest are being used to delay mercy. That's a reordering.
- **Obedience of faith:** quieter. She straightens and praises God. The response is worship, not a task.

Where it catches me: *she'd stopped expecting to be seen.* Eighteen years will do that.

---

## 03 — Exegetical (commentaries in, four works kept in view)

- **"A spirit had crippled her for eighteen years" (v.11); "whom Satan bound" (v.16).** Luke frames the bentness as bondage, not just illness — which is why "set free" (*apolyō* / *lyō*, the language of loosing) matters more than "healed". Keep that: the good news is *release*, not repair.
- **Sabbath setting (vv.14–16).** The leader cites the command rightly; Jesus doesn't deny the Sabbath, he reveals its purpose. The rabbinic allowance to untie (loose) an ox or donkey for water on the Sabbath is the hinge of Jesus' argument — you loose an animal for a drink, and you'd leave a daughter of Abraham bound? Same verb, *loose*, doing the theological work.
- **"Daughter of Abraham" (v.16).** Unusual phrase — confers full covenant dignity on a woman the system had pushed to the edge. Part of the worldview reordering.
- Commentators agree the structure pivots on a clash of two readings of the Sabbath: as restriction vs as the very day for liberation.

Does the exegesis change stage 02? It *sharpens* it: my instinct to say "healed" softens the text. The word is **set free**. Hold the line on that.

## 04 — Hermeneutical (the unified message)

> **Unified message: "Jesus sees the bent-down, and sets them free."** (7 words.)

How it emerges from the text: the passage's own movement is *seen → loosed → straightened → worship*, against a backdrop of a system that would delay mercy for the sake of order. The message doesn't import anything the text lacks — it names the text's own verbs.

The bridge to today: there are people in any room bent over by something long-term — illness, worry, shame — who've quietly stopped expecting to be noticed. The text says God notices first, and the noticing is the start of being set free. (Resisting the urge to make this "and here are five steps to deal with worry" — that would bend the text.)

What this is NOT about: it is not about the right way to keep the Sabbath, and it is not a self-improvement talk about managing anxiety. Both are good ideas. Both get cut.

## 05 — Homiletical (draft for the ear — excerpt)

> Unified message: "Jesus sees the bent-down, and sets them free."

**Preamble.** Eighteen years. Have a think about what you were doing eighteen years ago. That's how long this woman in our story had been bent over — couldn't straighten up at all. Not a bad week. Eighteen years.

**Into the text.** *(read Luke 13:10–13 slowly)* … Notice what comes first. She doesn't push to the front. She doesn't make her case. "When Jesus saw her, he called her over." He sees her first.

**Immediate response.** What do you notice in that? *(leave it. Let it sit.)*

**Core — viscerally.** I know a bit about being bent over. Not my back — though the van doesn't help. My first winter here, I didn't know a soul. I'd lie awake doing sums, working out what we couldn't pay for. And you stop looking up. You go about folded in on the worry. I couldn't have told you the last time I'd properly looked anyone in the eye.

There's a thing on the allotment — when you've not been down for a fortnight, everything's bolted and bent and gone over, and you almost don't want to look. This woman had been bent over for eighteen *years*. And the first thing that happens isn't a telling-off and isn't a to-do list. It's that she gets *seen*.

**The turn.** And the religious bloke in charge is cross — wrong day for it. And Jesus says: you'd untie your donkey to get it a drink on the Sabbath. This is a daughter of Abraham. Ought she not be set free? *Set free.* Not patched up. Loosed.

**Close.** *(quiet)* I wonder what you've been bent over by, so long you've stopped expecting anyone to notice. He sees you first. *(pause)* What would it be, for you, to straighten up?

**Four-works check:**
- **Encounter** — *present, well distributed.* "He sees her first" recurs; not back-loaded.
- **Good news** — *present.* "Set free… loosed" holds the release note the exegesis fought for.
- **Worldview transformation** — *weak.* The Sabbath-reordering ("the day is FOR this") is in the draft but underplayed; it's the thing that makes the freedom more than individual.
- **Obedience of faith** — *present, quiet.* Closes on a question (worship/standing-under), not a task. Good.

**The two tests:** Did they meet God today? **Yes.** Want to worship the God they meet? **Borderline-yes** — would be firmer if the worldview beat (Sabbath is for liberation) carried more weight.

**Drift caught (Constitution Part A — no assumed God-ward point):** an earlier draft of the close was *"so this week, try to notice the people around you who are bent over."* That's the **self-help drift** — strip God out and it still stands as a decent bit of advice about kindness. The fix wasn't to add God-language; it was to return the close to the text's own move: *he sees you first*, then the question. The God-ward point is now *earned* (the seeing is God's act), not assumed.

**Revision priorities (for the final cut):**
1. **Strengthen the worldview beat** — give one more breath to "the Sabbath is *for* setting people free", so the freedom isn't only private. (Lifts the borderline second test.)
2. **Length** — it's slightly long for 15 minutes; the allotment image and the donkey argument are both strong but the preamble can lose a sentence.
3. **Em-dashes** — count them in the final pass; several are doing pause-work that a full stop would do better.

**Verdict:** proceed to `07-final` with those three.
