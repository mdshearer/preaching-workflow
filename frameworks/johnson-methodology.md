## Core Theological Foundation

### "Something Always Happens" — The Central Conviction

Johnson's methodology rests on three interconnected claims:

1. When God speaks, something always happens.
2. When a preacher speaks God's Word, God speaks.
3. When a preacher speaks God's Word, therefore, something always happens.

**Critical insight:** This isn't optimistic wishful thinking — it's a conviction about the nature of God's Word as inherently transformative. The pressure is off the preacher to manufacture results; God's Word carries its own power.

### "Standing in the Mystery" Framework

**Core concept:** Preaching is participating in the mystery of God speaking through imperfect human vessels.

**Implications for preachers:**

- Takes pressure off being perfect or having all the answers
- Emphasises transformation as God's work, not ours
- Positions the preacher as participant, not producer
- Creates space for authentic vulnerability and ongoing learning

This framework gives theological grounding to a humble, learning-alongside posture rather than expert-above positioning.

## The Four Works Every Biblical Text Participates In

Every biblical text seeks to accomplish four works:

1. **Encounter** — an encounter with God in Jesus Christ
2. **Good News** — some aspect of the gospel message
3. **Worldview Transformation** — how we see reality differently
4. **Obedience of Faith** — response and faithful action

**Application:** Every reflection should help people experience all four, not just focus on one or two. The Critic stage checks each one (present / absent / weak) — a reflection can score well on voice and still fail theologically by neglecting two or more of these.

## Understanding as "Standing Under" (syniēmi)

**Greek word study:** *syniēmi* = to understand, literally "to stand under".

**Johnson's insight:** True biblical understanding requires willingness to *stand under* God's Word rather than standing over it as critic or analyst.

**Why some hear and others don't:**

- All four soils in Jesus' parable "hear" the word
- But only some choose to "stand under" its claim on their lives
- Understanding is not just intellectual comprehension but spiritual submission

This validates ending with questions rather than demanding immediate agreement — you're inviting people to "stand under" rather than forcing compliance.

## Seven Greek Verbs of Preaching

Johnson analyses seven different NT Greek verbs for preaching, each revealing different aspects.

***kēryssō*** (the most detailed example):

- Rooted in a noun for the messenger sent and authorised by the emperor
- The messenger speaks the emperor's message with the emperor's authority
- The preacher carries divine authorisation and a divine message

**Application:** Different contexts call for different "verbs" of preaching — from gentle teaching to bold proclamation to intimate conversation.

## Against Modernist Application Pressure

### Johnson's Strong Critique

> "The pressure to apply is a modernist pressure, not a biblical pressure."

**The problem with must-apply preaching:**

- Subtext: "You are gods unto yourselves… you can save yourselves by yourselves"
- Encourages people to "be perfected by the flesh"
- Makes transformation dependent on human effort rather than divine grace

### Johnson's Alternative Approach

> "If preachers are to give any imperative, any steps to take, their steps should be within the context of the text's own inherent implications."

**What this means:**

- Applications should emerge naturally from the text, not be imposed externally
- Trust the text's own transformative power
- Focus on God's work through the Word rather than human effort

This grounds an instinct toward questions over prescriptions and trust in community discernment over individual application.

## Oral Communication Principles

### Writing for the Ear, Not the Eye

Johnson borrows from G. Robert Jacks's *Just Say the Word*:

- "Write the way you talk, not the way you write."
- Speak in "breath bites" — manageable phrases for oral delivery.
- Preparation must account for how people hear, not just read.

### Preparation for Oral Impact

- Sermons are heard once in real time, not read and re-read.
- Natural speech patterns are more memorable than written prose.
- Rhythm and pacing matter as much as content.

This validates conversational, accessible language over academic formality.

## Process Framework Integration

The four classic moves of preparation, with Johnson's framing:

**Devotional Stage:**

- "Inhabiting the text" = living with the passage until it grabs you.
- "Standing under" the text rather than analysing it from above.
- Looking for encounter, good news, worldview shift, obedience call.

**Exegetical Stage:**

- Understanding historical and literary context.
- But always in service of the four works the text wants to accomplish.
- Not just information gathering but preparation for participation.

**Hermeneutical Stage:**

- How does this text want to transform our contemporary setting?
- What are the text's own inherent implications for today?
- Where is God already at work that this text reveals?

**Homiletical Stage:**

- How do we prepare for oral delivery?
- What structure serves the text's transformative purpose?
- How do we invite people to "stand under" rather than demanding compliance?

## Key Quotes That Shape the Approach

### On God's Work Through Imperfection

> "If it is all up to me to live a consistently whole and healthy spiritual life, then the dry bones of my dry soul will not live… If it is all up to you, me and all the other preachers alive today to bring about a moral revolution… then our world is doomed."

### On the Mystery of Preaching

> "The great mystery of preaching is that God speaks regardless (even if it's the worst sermon you have ever heard)."

### On Authentic Preaching

Johnson "liberates [preachers] to make use of their own personality, gifts and abilities as they communicate that message."

## How This Supports an Authentic Voice

### Theological Permission for Vulnerability

- "Standing in the mystery" validates not having all answers
- God works through imperfect proclamation
- Authentic personality is part of effective preaching

### Against Performance Pressure

- Transformation is God's work, not the preacher's performance
- "Something always happens" regardless of preacher perfection
- Focus on participation rather than production

### Conversational Authority

- Speaking as fellow learner rather than expert above
- Questions can be more faithful to the text than prescriptive applications
- Community discernment honours how God actually works

## Practical Applications for Reflections

### Pre-Sermon Questions (the four works, plus oral prep)

1. How is this text inviting people into encounter with God?
2. What good news does this text offer?
3. What worldview shift does this text call for?
4. What obedience of faith does this text inspire?
5. How can I prepare this for the ear, not just the eye?

### During Preparation

- Live with the text until it "grabs you".
- Ask what the text itself wants to accomplish.
- Prepare for oral delivery from the beginning.
- Trust the text's transformative power.

### Delivery Principles

- Speak conversationally, not academically.
- Use "breath bites" for oral clarity.
- End with questions that invite "standing under".
- Trust God to work through imperfect proclamation.
