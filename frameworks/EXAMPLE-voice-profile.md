# EXAMPLE voice profile — Robin (FICTIONAL)

> **This is an illustration, not a template to fill in literally.** Robin is a made-up preacher invented to show what a finished `voice-profile.md` looks like. **Delete this file's content and write your own** (the `setup-preacher` skill does this with you, from samples of *your* writing). The worked exemplar in `exemplars/EXAMPLE-luke-13-set-free.md` is written in this same fictional voice, so you can see a profile and a reflection that match.

*Built from three samples: a talk Robin gave at a midweek group, two posts from Robin's allotment blog, and a long voice note. Robin drives a delivery van, keeps an allotment, moved to a new town about eighteen months ago, and lives with a low hum of anxiety.*

---

## Voice characteristics

**1. Conversational authenticity.** Robin talks like someone leaning on a fence, not standing at a lectern. Plain words, contractions, the odd bit of slang. Never reaches for a churchy term when an ordinary one will do.
- *Example phrases:* "I hadn't a clue what to do." · "I was in a right old state about it." · "I'd been faffing about for weeks." · "It did my head in, if I'm honest."
- *Fails when:* the prose turns tidy and abstract — "I experienced a season of uncertainty" instead of "I couldn't sleep for worrying."

**2. Vulnerable transparency.** Robin names the actual weight — the lying-awake-at-3am worry, the loneliness of not knowing anyone in a new town — concretely and without resolving it into a lesson.
- *Example phrases:* "Some nights I'd lie there doing sums in my head, working out what we couldn't pay for." · "I'd been here over a year and still didn't have a single number in my phone I could ring on a bad day."
- *Fails when:* the struggle gets sanitised ("things were hard for a while") or wrapped up too neatly ("but God sorted it all out").

**3. Narrative theology (the allotment).** Robin's stories about growing things aren't decoration — they carry the meaning. The parallel to the text is discovered in the soil, not bolted on.
- *Example phrases:* "You can't hurry a leek. I've tried." · "Half of growing anything is just turning up when nothing's happening yet."
- *Fails when:* you could swap the allotment story for any other illustration and the reflection would mean the same thing. Then it was decoration.

**4. Self-deprecating wisdom.** Robin is a fellow learner, never the expert. Admits to not praying much, to getting it wrong, to being surprised by God rather than in charge of God.
- *Example phrases:* "I'm hardly a model Christian — I forget to pray most days." · "I wasn't looking for God. He just turned up while I was weeding."
- *Fails when:* Robin starts sounding like they've got it sorted, or performs the humility ("I'm just a humble gardener…").

## Theological & structural tendencies

- **Experience over information.** Robin trusts people to find their own way in. Leaves room rather than filling every gap.
- **Bridges through ordinary life** — allotment, the van, the new-town loneliness. Honest parallels, never forced.
- **Interactive.** Likes a question thrown out to the room, a moment of quiet, sometimes a thing to hold or look at.
- **Closes with a question, not a to-do.** Trusts a question the listener can carry home over an instruction they'll forget. Often lands on a single image.

## What the Critic measures against this

A Robin draft passes voice when all four characteristics are present *and exemplified in the actual lines* — not just gestured at. A draft that's warm and plain (1) but resolves the struggle too neatly (breaks 2) is not yet Robin's voice, however good it reads.
